A useful document for incident response: [Computer Security Incident Handling Guide]( https://csrc.nist.gov/pubs/sp/800/61/r2/final), which covers the entire lifecycle of incident handling
# Process
## Preparation
- Communication methods (know who you should contact on incident)
- Incident handling hardware & software (removable media, forensic software, ...)
- Incident analysis resources that helps during incident (documentation, security baselines, ...)
- Incident mitigation software (backup, clean images, ...)
- Policies and procedures, e.g. **IRP (Incident Response Plan)**, e.g. [Link](https://www.cyber.gov.au/sites/default/files/2023-03/ACSC-Cyber-Incident-Response-Plan-Template.docx)
## Detection
Detection is hard as large volumes of data come from the internet, one must know where to look
## Analysis
- Analysis of attacks helps in understanding the cause, process, potential results and probably solution
- Can be analyzed/scrutinized using IDS, IPS, Firewalls, Host-based monitoring tools, ...
## Containment
- Must isolate and contain the attack ASAP to limit damages
- Can use sandboxes to try malware in isolated environments (which may change his behavior)
## Eradication & Recovery
- Remove malware and any result of the attack (created accounts, ...)
- Fix vulnerabilities and entry points
- May use backups to recover from attack
## Lessons Learned
- Discuss improvement of system security and incident response handling in **the Post-Incident Meeting**.
- The Post-Incident Meeting should be conducted ASAP bcz memory fades, ...
- The post-incident meeting should cover the tough questions:
	- Identification & analysis of attack
	- Evaluation of incident response plan
	- What to improve in the incident response plan for next time
	- Update list of indicators to watch for in the future
# Training
- Must do incident response training before the incident
- Test both the people and the documentation (IRP)
# Testing
- Conduct test incidents on testing environment/infrastructure
- Must be efficient to avoid wasting a lot of money
## Tabletop exercise
- Assumption of the incident and then analysis through discussion around a table
- Less costly & time consuming
## Simulation
- Simulate event (phishing attacks, password changing requests, ...)
- Very efficient with phishing tests
# Root Cause Analysis
- Determining the root cause, original/ultimate cause of the incident
- Helps in improving security posture by addressing those root-causes

# Threat Hunting
- Stop/Patch the vulnerability before it is exploited
# Digital Forensics
A good set of best practice [RFC 3227: Guidelines for Evidence Collection and Archiving](https://datatracker.ietf.org/doc/html/rfc3227) (out of the scope of this course)
Digital data/artifacts can be demanded by law later
## [Legal Hold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_hold)
- Amounts of data & methods of data storing by the custodian generally specified by a lawyer.
- Can be held in a separate repository for **ESI (Electronically Stored Information)**
> A legal hold (or litigation hold) is a formal directive from an organization's legal team instructing employees to suspend routine document destruction and preserve all relevant, electronically stored information (ESI) and paper documents. Triggered by anticipated or pending litigation, investigations, or audits, it ensures evidence is not deleted or altered.
## [Chain of Custody](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_of_custody)
> Chain of custody is the chronological documentation or paper trail, showing the seizure, custody, control, transfer, analysis, and disposition of physical or electronic evidence.
- Track access to integrity-assured evidence
## Acquisition
- Data/Evidence can be obtained from multi-systems' RAM, disk, logs, ...
## Reporting
- Detailed reports of the data acquisition process to be used internally and for legal measures in the future.
- Reports must contain a summary, detailed explanations & analysis of the data acquisition, and a conclusion.
## Preservation
- Evidence must be stored (multiple copies!) for later use
- Must follow best practices to ensure admissibility of data in court.
## E-Discovery
E-discovery, or electronic discovery, is the legal process of identifying, preserving, collecting, processing, reviewing, and producing **ESI (Electronically Stored Information)** in response to a request for production in a lawsuit or investigation. It is a critical, often mandatory, part of modern litigation, as over 98% of information is created, transmitted, and stored electronically.
- Can work together with digital forensics to detect deleted data
