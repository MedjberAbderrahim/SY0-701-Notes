A **gap analysis** identifies the difference between an organization’s **current security posture** and its **desired state**. It’s a **continuous, structured process** that helps organizations plan improvements to meet security goals and adapt to evolving threats.
#### **Core Steps**
1. **Establish a Baseline:**  
    Define a reference point for current security using standards like **NIST SP 800-171** or **ISO/IEC 27001**.  
    The baseline covers **people** (skills, training), **processes** (policies, compliance), and **technology** (systems, controls).
2. **Assess Current State:**
    - Evaluate staff competence and security awareness.
    - Review policies, operational procedures, and compliance.
    - Analyze existing systems for **vulnerabilities**, **weak controls**, and **policy mismatches**.
3. **Break Down by Categories:**  
    Divide complex domains into smaller areas such as **access control**, **account management**, and **system monitoring** to pinpoint weaknesses precisely.
4. **Compare Against the Baseline:**  
    Map findings to frameworks (e.g., NIST controls) to see where the organization meets, partially meets, or fails requirements.
5. **Compile Findings:**  
    Create a detailed **report** highlighting:
    - Areas of compliance/non-compliance
    - **Critical gaps** and associated risks
    - **Recommendations** for remediation
6. **Develop a Plan Forward:**  
    Define **actions**, **responsible parties**, **costs**, and **timelines** to close gaps.  
    Reports often use **color-coded indicators** (Green/Yellow/Red) to prioritize effort by severity.
#### **Key Insights**
- Performing a gap analysis is **complex and time-consuming**, demanding coordination, data gathering, and management buy-in.
- The outcome isn’t just a report — it’s a **roadmap for security improvement**, guiding investment and compliance efforts.
- Regularly repeating the process ensures **continuous improvement** and **resilience** against new threats.
