### Definition & Importance
A threat actor is an entity causing security events that harm organizations or individuals. Recognized as malicious, understanding their traits helps in identifying motives, methods, and vulnerabilities for better defense.
### Attributes of Threat Actors
- **Origin:** Inside or outside the organization.
- **Resources & Funding:** From limited to extensive.
- **Sophistication:** From unskilled script kiddies to advanced developers.
- **Motivations:** Financial, political, ideological, or disruptive.
### Why Identification Matters
Knowing the attacker helps tailor defenses. For example, nation-state threats require different strategies than amateur hackers.
### Sources of Threat Actors
They can be:
- Insiders
- External actors like hackers, nation-states, or organized crime
### Resource & Skill Spectrum
- Limited resources & skills: Script kiddies using pre-made tools.
- High resources & sophistication: Nation-states or organized crime with advanced tools.
### Common Motivations
- Data theft (espionage, intelligence)
- Disruption (outages, damage)
- Political/ideological causes (hacktivism)
- Financial gain (ransomware, selling data)
- National security (military targets)
### Types of Threat Actors

#### Nation-State

**Traits:** Government-backed, well-funded, highly skilled.  
**Goals:** Data theft, influence, infrastructure disruption, geopolitical conflicts.  
**Examples:** APTs, Stuxnet.
#### Unskilled Attackers
Amateurs or script kiddies running simple scripts, motivated by curiosity or disruption. Limited skills and resources.
#### Hacktivists
Politically or ideologically driven, targeting websites for defacement, DDoS, or data leaks. Often skilled, may be supported financially, and can be external or internal.
#### Insider Threats
Internal individuals who intentionally or unintentionally compromise security. They have system knowledge and moderate skills, motivated by revenge, money, or coercion.
#### Organized Crime
Profit-driven groups with corporate structures, using ransomware, data theft, and other financial attacks. They have substantial resources.
#### Shadow IT
Departments or individuals bypassing official IT policies, creating unmanaged, insecure environments using unauthorized cloud services.
