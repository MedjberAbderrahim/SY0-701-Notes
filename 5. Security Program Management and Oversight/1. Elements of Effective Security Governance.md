# Guidelines
Rules (high level or detailed) established to maintain **CIA (Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability)**, they answer the ''what" & "why" of policies, whereas technical security controls answer the "how"
# Policies
- Companies should maintain a list of all security-related policies, some are even mandatory (compliance-related)
- Those policies can also include:
	- Procedures, e.g. **IRP (Incident Response Plan)**, **SOP (Standard Operating Procedures)**
	- Roles & responsibilities of security-related people
- Policies must be enforced
## Policies vs Guidelines
[Policies](https://www.google.com/search?client=ubuntu-sn&channel=fs&q=Policies&mstk=AUtExfDoDbfFwxEiyQZsbToSZzprugB9wD9dWNFc-hpl8RcTAhuhpfG5LO--pmt0VmML0PbueD4wyClqzhF7M57LmmO0NG17EiCVFHb01oElobAzWcXGubGGqpG70HSiX5DIEFgoLIpxinqtgtrZh-PxHt5-W8mQGlq2Ve5CnHgqMRjCXak&csui=3&ved=2ahUKEwiywqfIkvSSAxVKTqQEHbvaLrQQgK4QegQIARAE) are mandatory, formal, and legally binding rules within an organization that dictate required actions, with violations leading to discipline. [Guidelines](https://www.google.com/search?client=ubuntu-sn&channel=fs&q=Guidelines&mstk=AUtExfDoDbfFwxEiyQZsbToSZzprugB9wD9dWNFc-hpl8RcTAhuhpfG5LO--pmt0VmML0PbueD4wyClqzhF7M57LmmO0NG17EiCVFHb01oElobAzWcXGubGGqpG70HSiX5DIEFgoLIpxinqtgtrZh-PxHt5-W8mQGlq2Ve5CnHgqMRjCXak&csui=3&ved=2ahUKEwiywqfIkvSSAxVKTqQEHbvaLrQQgK4QegQIARAF), conversely, are optional, flexible recommendations or best practices designed to provide advice and guidance on how to perform a task, allowing for discretion and interpretation
## AUP (Acceptable Use Policies)
- Describes acceptable use of technology in the workspace by everyone
- Helps in holding legal liability against employees (officializing reason to fire them)
- Example AUP: https://frsecure.com/acceptable-use-policy-template/
## Business Continuity
- Must prepare & document compensating alternatives when digital systems fail
- Examples:
	- Manual transactions
	- Paper receipts
## Disaster Recovery
- A comprehensive, formal set of policies followed by everyone to ensure business continuity.
- Disasters are varied, the disaster recovery plan must include multiple measures, e.g. recovery location, data recovery methods, ...
## Security Incidents Response
- A formal set of policies defining handling of security incidents (DDoS, Malware, Data breach, ...)
- Should include multiple response-related roles:
	- Incident response team: specialized group, trained & tested
	- IT Security Management: Corporate support
	- Compliance Officers: Intricate knowledge for compliance rules
	- Technical staff: the ones fixing the technical issue
	- User Community: can provide useful info & feedback
- A useful document is [NIST SP 800-61 Rev. 2 "Computer Security Incident Handling Guide](https://nvlpubs.nist.gov/nistpubs/specialpublications/nist.sp.800-61r2.pdf), discussing the incident response lifecycle (already covered in 4.9)
## SDLC (Software Development LifeCycle)
A formal process for developing software in a methodical, organized manner, some of the most important methods are **Agile** & **Waterfall**

# Standards
- A formal definition with extensive documentation for using security technologies and processes
- Ensures everyone knows the security requirements and reduces security risk
- Some companies with very unique requirements use custom/internal standards, while most rely on international standards, e.g. **ISO (International Organization for Standardization)** & **NIST (National Institute of Standards and Technology)***
## Password
Every organization must implement authentication-related policies to ensure it aligns with appropriate standards, e.g. password length, password resets, local/domain account login in Active Directory, ...
## Access Control
Set policies to control access permissions to data and devices, e.g. which information, what time, discretionary vs mandatory, ...
May even include how a user can get access (must prior training/course, ...) and can remove that access from him (expiration, contracts, ...)
## Physical security
Policies regarding physical security controls, e.g. doors, employee access vs visitor access, ...
Can even include non-cybersecurity-related issues, such as off-boarding, mandatory escorts, ...
## Encryption
Specific standards for all things cryptographic, e.g. implementation standards, methods & techniques of password storage, data encryption standards for each type of data (at rest, in use, and in transit)
# Procedures
## Change Management
- Mentioned in detail in 1.3.1
- A formal process for managing change in the organization to avoid downtime, confusion, and mistakes
- Detailed process for implementing change, from determining scope and risk, to having a backup plan and documenting the change
- Example Change Management Document: https://www.uc.edu/content/dam/uc/ucit/docs/so/cm_process_document.pdf
## Onboarding/Offboarding
- **Onboarding:** The process of bringing someone new from outside or inside, IT agreements must be signed, new accounts created, appropriate IT hardware given.
- **Offboarding:** Processes to happen when an employee leave the organization, what happens to hardware, data, his user account (usually deactivated, not deleted), ...
## Playbooks
- A manual checklist of conditional steps to follow for processes
- Can be integrated into a **SOAR (Security Orchestration, Automation, and Response)** to increase efficiency through automation
## Playbooks vs SOPs
In cybersecurity, **[playbooks](https://www.google.com/search?client=ubuntu-sn&channel=fs&q=playbooks&ved=2ahUKEwjCuuTa5PKSAxX1OfsDHScZJK0QgK4QegQIARAC)** are high-level, strategic, and often automated workflows designed to manage specific, complex incidents (e.g., Ransomware Response). **[Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)](https://www.google.com/search?client=ubuntu-sn&channel=fs&q=Standard+Operating+Procedures+%28SOPs%29&ved=2ahUKEwjCuuTa5PKSAxX1OfsDHScZJK0QgK4QegQIARAD)** are detailed, rigid, step-by-step instructions for routine, specific tasks (e.g., resetting a user password or isolating a host)
# Monitoring and Revision
- Security always improving, must maintain updated security posture
- Update playbooks and procedures to protect against emerging threats
# Types of Governance Structures
## Boards
Panel of specialist, decision makers taking **broad, generic** decisions for the committees to implement
## Committees
Subject matter experts, may include a member of the board, decide the next steps to meet the vision/goals of the board and presents it to them for approval/change
## Government Entities
Generally present in the public sector, as policies/issues shift in governmental agencies to more legal, administrative, and political concerns
## Centralized/Decentralized
- Centralized governance is located in one location with a group of decision makers.
- Decentralized governance spreads the decision making process around to other individuals/locations
- There is no single "best" approach, always choose what suites the organization more
# External Considerations
## Regulatory
- Security regulations are often mandatory, e.g. logging, data storage, keeping emails a certain amount of years, ...
- Example regulations that many organizations are mandated to abide by:
	- [The Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarbanes%E2%80%93Oxley_Act) of 2002, which is the Public Company Accounting Reform and Investor Protection Act, which focuses on the finances of an organization, we can follow it as IT people is to well protect our financial data and make it available on demand to the right people.
	- [HIPAA (The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_Insurance_Portability_and_Accountability_Act): a US federal law protecting sensitive patient health information (PHI) from disclosure without consent. It mandates national standards for safeguarding medical records, applicable to healthcare providers, plans, and clearinghouses.
## Legal
Technical matters relating to law, e.g. reporting illegal activities, storing data required for legal proceedings, disclosure of security breaches.
Cloud computing made this harder because of Data Sovereignty and privacy issues (e.g. GDPR)
## Industrial
Regulations change from industry to industry, example difference:
- Public utilities and critical infrastructure (electrical power, ...) have very strict requirements for access control and isolation
- While medical environments must be highly available (not public!) and focus heavily on confidentiality
## Local/Regional
For organizations with local or regional focus, e.g. city and state government records
## National
For national, probably multi-state organization, shifts objectives to national defense, confidentiality (especially of national secrets)
## Global
For large multinational organizations, very complex to endeavor, as each country has its own rules and regulations
# Roles and Responsibilities for Systems and Data
## Data Owner
- Accountable and fully responsible for all the data associated with a certain role, often a senior officer, e.g.
	- VP of Sales owns the customer relationship data, ...
	- Treasurer owns the financial information
- Senior Executive. **Accountable** for the data. They define the classification level (Secret, Public, etc.).
## Data Controller
Manages how the data will be used, gives instructions to the data processor, i.e. the purposes and means by which personal data is processed
## Data Processor
The one actually processing/using the data, often third-party or a different group
Example:
- Payroll department is the data controller, defines payroll amounts and timeframes
- Payroll company processes the payroll and store employee information
## Data Custodian
- Responsible for data accuracy, security, and privacy
- Ensures compliance to data-related regulations
- classifies the data according to sensitivity levels, manages its access rights and implements security controls
- IT Staff/Admin. **Responsible** for the technical implementation (backups, access rights) based on the Owner's rules.
