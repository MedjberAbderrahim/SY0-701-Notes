IT changes in large organizations need a formal process to ensure stability, security, and minimal disruption. Unlike personal changes, these require standard procedures.
### Why Formal Processes Matter
They help:
- Maintain uptime
- Prevent uncoordinated failures
- Inform stakeholders
- Reduce security risks
Without control, changes can cause issues and downtime.
### Change Approval Process
-  Complete the request forms.
-  Determine the purpose of the change.
-  Identify the scope of the change.
-  Determine affected systems and impact.
-  Analyze the risk associated with the change.
-  Get approval from the change control board.
-  get end-user acceptance (satisfaction) after the change is complete.
### Change Control Steps
Typical process:
#### 1. Request
Submit a form with reason, affected systems, and schedule.
#### 2. Risk & Impact
The **Change Control Board (CCB)** assesses risk, impact, and timing.
#### 3. Approval
CCB approves or rejects based on risks and benefits.
#### 4. Implementation & Testing
Schedule, test functionality, and validate with stakeholders.
#### 5. Contingency
Prepare rollback plans, backups, and test procedures.
### Stakeholders
The ones being impacted by the change, application owners initiate requests, test, and validate. Multiple departments may be involved.
### Risk & Impact
Risks include failures, data issues, security gaps, and disruptions. Risks guide testing and approval levels.
### Testing Environments
Use sandboxes to identify issues, test rollbacks, and ensure compatibility.
### Backout Procedures
Have plans to revert changes, including backups and rollback scripts.
### Timing & Scheduling
Schedule during off-hours or maintenance windows to minimize impact, especially in 24/7 setups.
### Documentation & Policies
Maintain clear policies on procedures, testing, rollback, roles, and responsibilities.
### Continuous Improvement
Regularly review and update processes based on lessons learned.
