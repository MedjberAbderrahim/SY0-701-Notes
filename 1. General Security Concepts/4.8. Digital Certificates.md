Digital certificates are electronic IDs stored in files, containing:
-  Public Key: for encryption and signature verification
-  Digital Signature: confirms authenticity and integrity
-  Metadata: serial number, issuer, subject, etc.

They establish trust by verifying identities and enabling secure communication.

### The Role of Trust
Trust is key in IT security, especially for access control. Certificates link entities to their public keys, backed by trusted authorities.

Trust is built through:
-  Digital signing by a Certificate Authority (CA)
-  Web of trust models with mutual signing
-  Internal CAs within organizations

### Certificate Authorities & Trust Models
A CA is a trusted third party that issues and signs certificates, verifying identities. Trust is established via:
-  Validation by the CA
-  Digital signatures on certificates
-  Inclusion of CA certificates in browsers and OS

Alternatively, a web of trust allows decentralized signing among users.
### Creating & Validating Certificates
Steps include:
1. Generate key pair
2. Create CSR with identity info and public key
3. Submit CSR to CA
4. CA verifies and signs the certificate
5. Install the certificate

>This process ensures trust through CA validation.
### Internal CAs

Organizations can set up internal CAs with tools like Windows Certificate Services or OpenCA, reducing reliance on external CAs. They involve:
-  Installing CA software
-  Distributing the root certificate
-  Signing internal certificates

>Trust depends on the internal CA's root certificate being trusted.
### Certificate Details & Extensions

Certificates include:
-  Serial Number & Version
-  Signature Algorithm
-  Issuer & Subject
-  Public Key
-  Subject Alternative Name (SAN): supports multiple domains, including wildcards (e.g., *.domain.com)

>SANs simplify managing multiple domains with one certificate.
### Revocation & Management

Certificates are revoked if:
-  Servers are decommissioned
-  Keys are compromised
-  Vulnerabilities are found (e.g., Heartbleed)

Revocation is managed via:
-  CRL: list of revoked certificates
-  OCSP: real-time status checking

>OCSP allows browsers to verify certificate status during SSL handshakes.
### Revocation Protocols

Most browsers support OCSP, which involves:
-  Embedding OCSP responses (stapling)
-  Querying OCSP responders directly

>This helps prevent use of revoked certificates. Some older systems may lack OCSP support.
