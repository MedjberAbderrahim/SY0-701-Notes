# Risk identification
Risk management is part of any growing organization, helps understand potential risks (external vs internal, ...) to get ahead of any potential problems, and analysis of risk helps plan for contingencies
# Risk Assessment
Risk is handled/managed differently depending on organization, so are risk assessments
## One-time
Event-driven risk assessments, generally used for specific cases, i.e. one time things, e.g. a risk assessment specific to a project about to launch, a company acquiring another company, ...
## Continuous
Provides real-time, automated monitoring of threats for immediate response, essential for dynamic environments, reducing resource burdens through automation and identifying emerging threats faster.
## Ad Hoc
Sudden risk assessment when an event occurs (CEO comes back from conference with new threat intelligence intel), a committee is created (if doesn't already exist), and after that it's disbanded
## Recurring
Based on standard intervals (3 months, 6 months, ...), sometimes mandated by regulations, e.g. PCI DSS requires annual risk assessment.

## Quantitative
Yields values as results, often uses some key terms:
- **ARO (Annualized Rate of Occurrence)**: how likely it is to happen per year
- **AV (Asset Value)**: General cost of the asset's loss, i.e. replacement, possible fines, reputation, ..
- **EF (Exposure Factor)**: A percentage number (0 to 1) of how much of the asset will be lost, e.g. quarter = 0.25
- **SLE (Single Loss Expectancy)**: Monetary loss if a **single** event occurs:
	- **Formula**: **AV (Asset Value)** x **EF (Exposure Factor)**
	- **Example**: Laptop Stolen
```
$1,000 (AV) (The laptop, its data, ...) x 1.0 (EF) (Entirely lost) = $1,000 (SLE)
```
- **ALE (Annualized Loss Expectancy)**: total annual loss of the event:
	- **Formula**: **SLE (Single Loss Expectancy)** x **ARO (Annualized Rate of Occurence)**
	- **Example**: 7 Laptops stolen a year
```
7 (ARO) x $1,000 (SLE) (Calculated earlier) = $7,000 (ALE)
```
### Quantitative vs Qualitative
- **Quantitative:** Uses numbers and dollar signs (SLE, ALE, ARO). It is objective
- **Qualitative:** Uses "gut feelings" or scales (Low, Medium, High). It is subjective.

If you find a factor that you _cannot_ easily put a dollar sign on (like "reputational damage" or "employee morale"), it usually shifts into the **Qualitative** side of the assessment. However, if you can estimate that reputational damage will cost the company **$50,000** in lost sales, you can bring it back into your **Quantitative** SLE calculation.
And that's why companies use them **both**.
## Impact
- Life: always biggest priority, assets can be replaced, people can't (Legal Liability, Ethics, and Irreparable Loss)
- Property: buildings, assets, ...
- Safety: safety impact to individuals and the company itself
- Financial: financial cost
## Likelihood and Probability
- **Risk Likelihood**: A **qualitative** value of the occurrence rate of an event
- **Risk Probability**: A **quantitative** value, statistical measurement (0-100%), can be based on historical performance of the occurrence rate of an event.
- They can be used interchangeably or in-relation to each other
![[Pasted image 20260226105132.png]]
### Risk Probability vs ARO (Annualized Rate of Occurrence)
- **ARO (Annualized Rate of Occurrence)**: a **count** of how many times an event happens within a strictly defined window of **one year**, not limited to 1.0. It can be 0.1 (once every 10 years), or it can be 50 (nearly once a week), considered a "math-ready" variable for budgeting.
- **Risk Probability**: a **statistical chance** that an event will occur, strictly bound between **0 and 1** (0% to 100%).
- **How they work together**: In a real-world quantitative assessment, you often use the **Probability** to find the **ARO**.
> **Example:** If you have 1,000 employees traveling this year, and the **Probability** of a laptop being stolen during a trip is **1%** (0.01):
> 1,000 (events)×0.01 (probability)=10 (ARO)
> Now you have your ARO (10) to plug into your **ALE (Annualized Loss Expectancy)** formula.

# Risk Tolerance
- A **quantitative, specific** of fluctuation a company can handle around a specific objective. It is the "line in the sand" where the Risk Appetite meets reality.
## Risk Tolerance vs Risk Appetite
### Example 01
- **Risk Appetite**: "To be the fastest-growing fintech startup, we have a **high risk appetite** for adopting cutting-edge, unproven cloud technologies"
- **Risk Tolerance:** "While we have a high appetite for cloud tech, our **risk tolerance** for system downtime is **strictly 0.05% (about 4 hours per year)**. If we hit 4.1 hours, we are in the 'unacceptable' zone."
### Example 02
Imagine you are going on a road trip.
- **Risk Appetite:** You decide, "I want to get there as fast as possible, so I'm willing to drive fast." (This is your **Appetite** for risk).
- **Risk Tolerance:** You look at the 65 mph speed limit and decide, "I’m okay going up to **72 mph**. If I hit 73 mph, I’m slowing down because the risk of a ticket is too high." (This is your **Tolerance**—the specific threshold).

# Risk Management Strategies
Each organization can handle risk differently, depending on cost, policies, risk appetite, ...; the actions generally are:
- **Transfer**: Move the risk under the control of another party, e.g. buying some cybersecurity insurance
- **Accept**: usually the most common course of action, can be redirected to another decision later, for its variants:
	- **Exempt**: Exempt the risk from the company's internal policies and regulations, for the lack of an acceptable solution, e.g. device CANNOT be patched, which goes against company's policy of patching
	- **Except**: Temporarily avoid the company's policy for some special cases, e.g. Latest patches of device are incompatible, so IT must take some time  to fix conflicts before updating, an exception to the company's internal policy of patching within 3 days.
	- **Exempt vs Except**: An **Exemption** is a formal, documented, and often long-term waiver that allows a specific entity to bypass a security policy or control. While an **Exception** is a deviation from a policy for a **specific instance** or a **limited timeframe**. It’s usually a "just this once" scenario to handle a temporary business need or an emergency.
- **Avoid**: Remove (or more accurately "avoid") the risk by removing its root-cause (the high-risk activity).
- **Mitigate**: Decrease/minimize the risk level
# Risk Reporting
A constantly-updated formal document identifying risks and describing them in detail, generally for upper management to make the right business decisions regarding resources, budgeting, ...
# Business Impact Analysis
## RTO (Recovery Time Objective)
Measures the maximum tolerable downtime to restore business systems, focusing on speed of recovery
## RPO (Recovery Point Objective)
Measures the maximum acceptable data loss, measured backward from the failure, focusing on data freshness
## MTTR (Mean Time To Repair)
Average time required to fix an issue as a whole, including diagnosing, replacing, patching, ...

## MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures)
The estimated time that the system will run before another outage, helps determining outage times, can be provided by manufacturer, based on historical performance (total uptime / number of breakdowns)
